\section{Radar Processing}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Radar processing converts received echoes into target measurements such as range, velocity, and angle.
    \item Radar hardware performs transmission, reception, amplification, pulse-compression, downconversion, and analogue-to-digital conversion, while software extracts useful target information from sampled data.
    \item In software, a typical processing chain begins by organising the pulse-compressed samples across fast time, slow time, and antenna channels.
    \item By considering the samples the spectral domain (either with matched filtering or a fast time Fourier Transform), we can estimate the range of targets. 
    \item Optionally, a long time Fourier transform can be applied across slow time to estimate Doppler, which provides target radial velocity and helps separate moving targets from clutter. 
    \item Coherent integration across multiple pulses improves detectability of weak targets. This involves summing the complex samples across pulses.
    \item After integration detection algorithms such as Constant False Alarm Rate (CFAR) can identify candidate target returns, which are then passed to angle estimation and tracking stages. 
    \item The bearing and elevation of targets can be estimated using monopulse processing, which compares the received signal across multiple antenna channels to infer angle with high accuracy.
    \item The resulting range, Doppler, and angle measurements form the basis for later tracking and classification stages in a DAA system.
\end{itemize}

Every radar system operates by transmitting electromagnetic waves into an environment and 
receiving the reflected signals to detect and locate targets, (Figure \ref{fig:radar_operation}). 
The strength of the received signal is given by the equation,
\begin{equation}
P_r = \frac{P_t G_t G_r \lambda^2 \sigma}{(4\pi)^3 R^4 L}
\end{equation}
where $P_r$ is the received power, $P_t$ is the transmitted power, $G_t$ and $G_r$ are the gains 
of the transmitting and receiving antennas, $\lambda$ is the wavelength, $\sigma$ is the radar 
cross-section of the target, $R$ is the range to the target, and $L$ represents system losses.

Hence, the received signal informs us about the target's radar cross-section, reflectivity and 
range, while the time delay between transmission and reception gives us just range. The 
Doppler shift of the received signal provides information about the target's radial 
velocity. If there are multiple receiving channels, the relative phase and amplitude of 
the received signals across channels can be used to estimate the bearing and elevation of 
the target.

To measure the range of targets, radar systems need to transmit the signal as pulses. Pulses are 
generally preferred over continuous constant frequency signal because the time difference between
the transmitted pulse and the received echo can be easily measured to estimate range. The 
transmitted signal is often modulated in frequency or phase. Linearly frequency modulation,
(LFM) creates pulses that involve a continuous sweep of frequencies during the pulse duration
(see Figure \ref{fig:LFM}). Advantages of such a modulation include reduced sensitivity to 
frequency dependent interference, and improved range resolution due to the effect of pulse compression.
Through pulse compression, the long duration LFM pulse can be compressed in time to illustrate 
the difference between the transmitted and received signals. One pulse compression technique
for LFMCW signals is described as "de-chirping", which involves mixing (multiplying) the received 
signal with the transmitted signal to produce a "beat" frequency that is proportional to the 
range of the target (See Figure \ref{fig:de-chirping}). The resulting signal's frequencies are given by the product of cosines
identity,
\begin{equation}
    \cos A \cos B = \frac{1}{2} [\cos(A-B) + \cos(A+B)]
\end{equation}
where $A$ is the transmitted signal and $B$ is the received signal. By applying a low-pass filter 
to the mixed signal, we obtain the lower frequency component, which is the beat frequency. 
The beat frequency is directly proportional to the range of the target, allowing us to estimate the 
target's distance from the radar with a precision much high than the estimate obtained from the 
time of flight.

For radar systems where the receiver is close to the transmitter, the transmitter signal can leak
into the receiver, creating a strong interference signal that can cover weak pulse echoes from 
targets. To mitigate this, radar systems rapidly switch between transmission and reception modes 
so that the transmitter isn't active when receiving. 

The compressed pulses are sampled at a lower rate than the original signal, capturing the important
beat frequency information.
